The world is relying more and more on plastics. As evident in this graph, we can see an exponential increase in the amount of plastics produced, this can mean drastic impacts on the environment and the health of people as well. This sudden increase in plastics over the last 50 years has played a dramatic role in human health, with the introduction of BPA free products, studies showed that the toxic effects were minimised but not completely eradicated.

Studies in 2015 have shown that the increased use of plastics in our kitchens and general life have contributed to the consumption of up to 5 grams of micro plastics a week, which is the equivalent of a credit card a week. The consumed micro plastics have negative effects in the long term, which includes the gradual decrease in male reproduction and sperm quality, making them a potential hazard to reproductive success. This decrease in male reproduction directly correlates with the graph of the global plastics production.

What are Micro-Plastics?

Microplastics are plastic debris pieces that are smaller than five millimeters in size. They can be found in most habitats on Earth as well as in the digestive tracts of many marine organisms and sea birds. This is alarming us to take action.

Countries With the Highest Plastic Waste Per Capita





Comparing plastic waste per capita (kg/person/day) is a better way to distinguish which countries are doing the most damage. When comparing the percentage of plastic waste by country, the country's size and population often impacts its wastage. A larger country might have more plastic waste but their per capita waste is more fair.
